Expressive Storytelling with Timelines

Support for chronological or non-chronological narratives,

Choice of representations for time,

Choice of time scales.
Existing tools also tend towards a chronological narrative: either the whole timeline is shown up front, so readers are likely to begin at the start of the timeline, or events are revealed in chronological order.

For some stories, a chronological introduction of events makes total sense, while for others it does not,

Like in my painters example, I revealed the career of Matisse after I showed you Dali's, which added context to his career.

To achieve this expressive narrative design, we can make use of animation, highlighting, and annotation to incrementally reveal parts of a narrative (that may not be chronological), to allow the viewer to make new comparisons.

Timeline Storyteller

Produce visually expressive timeline narratives in the browser or in Microsoft Power BI.
timelinestoryteller.com
So I set about building and deploying an authoring tool so that anyone could tell expressive timeline stories and make use of our design space without having to program these stories from scratch.

This effort culminated in Timeline Storyteller, an open-source and freely available interactive authoring tool for the web and for Microsoft's Power BI application that we released in mid 2017.

You could think of it as PowerPoint for timeline data.

The Daily Routines of Famous Creative People

Inspired by infographics by Podio and info we trust.
Data source: Daily Rituals: How Artists Work by Mason Currey (2013)
As a second example that ties all of this together, I'm going to present a story about the daily routines of famous creative people.

MAX: What you see here is a set of radial timelines depicting a typical 24 hours in the lives of 26 writers, artists, composers, and the like.

When they work, eat, sleep, exercise, and do other activities.

NEXT: A good starting point is to ask: when do creative people create? Are people similar in this regard?

It looks as though some people prefer to work in short, focused bursts, like Darwin or Kant, while people like Murakami and Voltaire get up early and work for long uninterrupted periods of time.

Meanwhile, you have people like Kafka who work all night long.

NEXT: We can also ask about the relationship between sleep and creativity: how much? how often? and when do creative people sleep?

Most people in this dataset sleep at night, with notable exceptions like Balzac who would turn in at 6pm, and Kafka, who just seemed to keep odd hours.

What about variation and creativity? How often should you change activities if you want to be creative?

NEXT: This chronological scale isn't the best way to convey the number or heterogeneity of activities, so let's transition to a sequential scale.

NEXT: To determine who varied the most and least, a linear representation is perhaps best, where we can easily see that Darwin has the most varied day of all the people in this dataset,

while Murakami prefers less variation.

NEXT: Finally, we can restore chronology to compare these timelines just by scanning up and down, to spot synchronocities like who tends to work or sleep at the same time.

And this is a good design choice to finish the story with, to invite the viewer to compare their own daily rhythm to these creative people.

Evaluating Timeline Storyteller

A laboratory study to assess expressivity seemed to be inappropriate.

How do people use it with their own data?

How does the content they produce reflect our design space?
Since its release in mid 2017, I've put considerable amount of my time and effort into promoting and demonstrating Timeline Storyteller, as well as understanding how people are using it.

For an design tool such as Timeline Storyteller, a controlled user study in a lab measuring task completion time and error didn't seem appropriate.

I was instead interested in finding out how people would express themselves with their own data and on their own time,

And to determine if our timeline design space and the ideas put forward in our journal paper would be reflected in what people make.

Timeline Storyteller: Usage

We held a data storytelling contest with the Power BI user community.

We also collected exported content and tracked usage metrics.
Next came the challenge of finding out how people use Timeline Storyteller.

First, we ran a storytelling contest within the Power BI community, where participants would post a timeline story created with Timeline Storyteller, which we and members of the journalism team would judge.

and a winning entry that incrementally revealed the history of the highest rated television shows over the course of past few decades, and this story was richly annotated and hit several points in our design space.

Timeline Storyteller: Content Analysis

We examined exported content from the web version (subject to author consent).

This resulted in a corpus of 223 unique artifacts.

This corpus spanned our timeline design space (with a couple of exceptions),

Though the Linear representation and Chronological scale were most common.
Second, we saved copies of content exported from the web version of Timeline Storyteller over the course of several months, subject to the approval of content authors.

After reviewing all of this content and discarding duplicate versions of content, we identified 223 unique artefacts generated with Timeline Storyteller, which included static images, animated GIFs, and multi-scene stories.

And what we found was that nearly every point in our timeline design space was represented in this corpus, though most of this corpus incorporated a linear representation and a chronological time scale.

Timeline Storyteller: Usage Metrics

Over 36,000 downloads of the Power BI version (as of Dec 2018).

Over 51,000 views of our YouTube tutorial (as of Jan 2018).

156 stars of Github repository (as of Jan 2018). Tracking issues at: github.com/Microsoft/timelinestoryteller/ | timelinestoryteller@microsoft.com
Finally, we had coarse quantitative metrics.

Since Power BI is a desktop product and has different protocols for data collection and retention than Microsoft Research, we don't know as much about how people use it unless they contact us directly.

However, we do know that by the end of 2018, the Timeline Storyteller component for Power BI had been downloaded over 36,000 times.

Timeline Storyteller: Lessons Learned

You can never have enough tutorial content and design inspiration.

We could recommend design choices and annotations.

The viewing experience ought to be more responsive.
Over the course of this long-term deployment and analysis of its usage, we've come to appreciate that you can never have enough example datasets, design inspiration, and tutorial content.

We also saw the potential for surfacing design recommendations more overtly within the interface, for recommending annotations.

And finally, we now have a better appreciation for responsive design, as the experience of viewing Timeline Storyteller content from a mobile device is certainly lacking, which foreshadows the second topic area that I'll focus on today.
