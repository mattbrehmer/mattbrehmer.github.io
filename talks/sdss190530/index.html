Our Expressive Visualization Tools for Storytelling

· Not intended for exploratory data analysis

· Authoring, not design

· Correctly formatted, clean data

· Interactive browser-based tools: no programming required

· Anticipating alternative export and re-use scenarios


· Available to the public, free, open-source*

 *DataToon public / open-source release is forthcoming.

Before I speak about the individual tools, I'll outline some of their commonalities.

While the term "storytelling" can be overloaded, the term is useful when distinguishing data analysis tools and presentation or visual communication tools.

Our tools assume that when you've already done your exploratory data analysis via some other means and identified some finding or insight that you want to communicate.

They also assume that your data is clean and correctly formatted.

Furthermore, these tools assume that you have a visualization design in mind. They are not visualization recommender systems.

Instead, they are visualization authoring tools, allowing you to realize your intended visualization design.

And they are interactive tools, intended either for people who don't have the time or experience to employ a programmatic approach to visualization such as D3 or Vega.

These tools also allow you to easily export content, in all cases as static images, and in some cases as interactive presentations, as animated GIFs, or as reusable content that can be ingested in Microsoft's Power BI software.

Finally, with the exception of DataToon, the first three tools I'll present are open-source and freely available for you to use in your browser. I believe that a public DataToon release is forthcoming.

Considerations for Storytelling with Visualization

· Fine control over visual encoding choices

· Informative annotations and captions

· Selective emphasis and highlighting

· Sequential reveal of information over time and space

These tools draw upon previous research and best practices for storytelling with visualization.

This means giving you fine control over visual encoding choices, or the mapping from a data point to a visual representation as a mark on a canvas.

This means giving you the ability add informative annotations and captions,

To selectively emphasize or highlight content to direct your viewers' attention,

Or to sequentially reveal information over time or space.

The annotation layer is most important when it comes to communicating information with charts.

Otherwise, the reader has to figure out what to make of it.

The ChartAccent project is certainly motivated by this assertion,

And draws substantial inspiration from how major newsrooms annotate their charts.

So what is annotation?

Annotating Points and Values

Annotation may involve labels, leader lines, and other forms of emphasis applied to data-bound marks, or particular values along axes,

Like you see here with the freezing point.

Annotating Ranges

Or you may annotate spans along an axes,

Or aggregate values, such as the average temperature value for the Chicago series in this lien chart.

Annotating Sets and Other Annotations

You can also annotate sets of marks in addition to individual marks.

Or annotate other annotations, such as the text and arrow pointing to the line of best fit.

The Daily Routines of Famous Creative People

Story inspired by infographics by Podio and info we trust (RJ Andrews).
Data source: Daily Rituals: How Artists Work by Mason Currey (2013)
To give you an example of a way to present timelines, I want to show you a brief presentation about the daily routines of famous creative people.

MAX: What you see here is a set of radial timelines depicting a typical 24 hours in the lives of 26 writers, artists, composers, and other creative types.

When they work, eat, sleep, exercise, and do other activities.

NEXT: I had wondered if there was any pattern as to when creative people do their creative work.

It turns out that at least among these people, some creative people prefer to work in short, focused bursts, like Darwin or Kant, while people like Murakami and Voltaire get up early and work for long uninterrupted periods of time.

Meanwhile, you have odd ones like Kafka who work all night long.

NEXT: I had also wondered about the potential relationship between sleep and creativity, so let me focus your attention to these blue arcs.

Most people here tend to sleep at night, with notable exceptions like Balzac who would turn in at 6pm, and Kafka, who just seemed to keep odd hours.

A third question I had was about variation and creativity. Let me show you how often these creative people change activities throughout their day.

NEXT: This 24-hour clock metaphor isn't the best way to convey the number or heterogeneity of activities, so I'm going to transition to a just show you a sequence of activities without any duration, effectively resulting in a radial bar chart.

NEXT: To show who varied the most and least among these people, a linear representation is probably better, where you can see that Darwin has the most varied day of all the people here while Murakami prefers less variation.

NEXT: Finally, I'll restore the duration of events so that you can compare these timelines just by scanning up and down, where you might spot times where some routines appear to be in sync with one another, such as when people work or sleep at the same time.

And this is a good time to ask you to compare your own daily rhythm to these creative people.

When do you sleep, eat, or do you best creative work?

Representation

Scale

It turns out that there are many ways to present timelines.

You can represent time in various ways: linearly, radially, or by using spirals, grids such as calendars, or along a curve

And you can combine these representations with different time scales: you could use an absolute or relative chronological scale, a logarithmic scale for extremely long-spanning timelines, or you could use a purely sequential scale.

And then there's "Layout", or how to draw one or more timelines within a page or display:

This has to do with deciding between a single timeline, faceting into multiple timelines (like how I did for each creative person),

Or wrapping a single timeline into meaningful segments of time, like a decade or a century.
Different combinations of representation, scale, & layout
serve different communicative goals.

Anticipate chronological or non-chronological narratives.

Incrementally reveal, selectively highlight & annotate to direct attention.

timelinestoryteller.com | interactive tool for the browser or Power BI

github.com/Microsoft/timelinestoryteller | open source

openvisconf.com/2017 | What Story Does Your Timeline Tell?
In this IEEE TVCG paper I make the case that timeline design tools should provide alternative representations of time, time scales, and layouts,

As different combinations of these serve different communicative tasks.

For instance, sometimes I want to emphasize sequence without duration, other times periodicity.

I also discuss in this paper how existing timeline tools also tend towards a chronological narrative:

Either the whole timeline is shown as a static image, so readers are likely to begin at the start of the timeline, or events are revealed in chronological order, one event at a time as a series of slides.

For some stories, a chronological introduction of events makes total sense, while for others it does not,

Like how in my story about the routines of creative people, I revealed and highlighted aspects of those timelines in an order that wasn't chronological starting at midnight.

To achieve this expressive narrative design, you should make use of animation, highlighting, and annotation to incrementally reveal parts of a narrative (that may not be chronological), to allow the viewer to make new comparisons.

Each of these considerations were realized in the tool Timeline Storyteller.

Reflecting on the Evaluation of Visualization Authoring Systems.
Ren, Lee, Brehmer, & Henry Riche.
In Proceedings of the 2018 BELIV Workshop (Evaluation & Beyond).
Typical evaluation metrics (time, error) are not very relevant.

Post-deployment content analysis and chart reproduction studies.

aka.ms/renbeliv18

In the course of developing these visualization tools over the past few years,

My collaborators and I have also put some thought into how we evaluate tools like these.

With visual analysis tools, there are often benchmark datasets and analysis tasks, and human factors studies can measure task completion time and error rates.

However, task completion time and error rate don't apply so well to visualization authoring tools.

Instead, we're mainly interested in expressivity, or whether a wide gamut of visualization design choices can be reached within these tools.

Given this focus on expressivity, the evaluation of tools like these often involves think-aloud chart reproduction studies, where we try to understand all the possible ways that a particular design can be achieved,

We also tend to publicly deploy these tools as a way to better understand how people use them with their own data and on their own time,

The challenge here can be in engaging with the community, encouraging people to share what they create in contests or on social media channels; we've tried both in the case of Timeline Storyteller and Charticulator.

Closing Thoughts / Looking to the Future

· What does expressivity mean? How do we achieve it?

· Maximize expressivity without programming / mouse + keyboard?

· Visualization authoring for consumption beyond the desktop

· Beyond authoring: supporting design, exploration, data prep

Looking to the future, my colleagues and I will continue to question what expressivity means in the context of visualization tools for communication and storytelling.

How can we push the boundaries of expressivity, particularly as we move away from keyboard and mouse authoring?

And can we assist people in authoring content intended for different consumption contexts, from large presentation displays to mobile phone screens.

And finally, the grand challenge is to consider ways to integrate other parts of the data analysis and visualization workflow into single tools: authoring, design, exploratory data analysis, and data preparation.
