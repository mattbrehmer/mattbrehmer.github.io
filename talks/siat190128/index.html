WHY EXPRESSIVITY?

So why is expressivity important?

And what does expressivity mean in the context of visualization and information design?

<< CLICK >> First of all, it's about being to address a wide range of tasks, and by this I refer to both the tasks of a visualization producer or designer, as well as the tasks of a viewer, or what the audience is expected to do with an information graphic.

Ultimately, expressivity is about the intent of the designer (the message or story they want to tell) and whether this story can be reconstructed in the mind of the viewer.

It would seem as though the visualization community has appreciated this effort and has found it to be useful for speaking about the expressiveness of visualization, since my task typology paper written with my PhD advisor Tamara Munzner has gone on to be the most cited InfoVis paper since 2013, with more than 280 citations.

Visualization Design Choices

Expressivity also refers to a palette of design choices: of visual representation choices, or interaction design choices, of ways to highlight, annotate, and embellish information graphics, and of ways to combine visual and textual explanations in compelling and engaging ways.

And for each of these design choices, there is a question about the level of granularity: for example, as a designer are you controlling low-level visual encoding channels like shape or color, or are you picking a chart type from a template?

Constraints on Expressivity

Expressivity is also bound to constraints, and it is these constraints that I find to be particularly challenging and worthy of research.

Expertise Constraints

One constraint is expertise: how can we empower non-programmers to visualize data? Or how can we empower non-designers to make compelling and useful information graphics?

Limited Resources Constraints

Another constraint is resources: how can we empower overcommitted, understaffed, and poorly funded local news teams to produce data-driven stories about their community while meeting all their deadlines?

Or how can we empower professors and teachers to produce visual explanations for all of their lectures?

Context Constraints

Yet another constraint is context: what if your audience is consuming your content from a mobile device?

Or what if your audience lacks the sufficient visual literacy or data literacy to interpret your favourite chart type?

Democratizing Data Visualization

Addressing these constraints serves the larger goal of democratizing data visualization: enabling more people to visualize data and tell stories.

Visualizing Personally-Relevant Data

It's also about making data visualization more personal; allowing people to visualize data that is personally relevant to them and situated in the right context, whether that be on a mobile phone, in a lecture hall, or in a public institution.

Connecting Research and Practice

VisInPractice.github.io (practitioner speaker series at IEEE VIS).

TIMELINES REVISITED

Timelines Revisited: A Design Space and Considerations for Expressive Storytelling.
Brehmer, Lee, Bach, Riche, and Munzner. In IEEE TVCG 2017.
In late 2014 I became arguably obsessed by timelines and the potential for expressive information design with this form of data: annotated and categorized sequences of events used to tell stories in the domains of journalism, education, and project management.

I began collecting and systematically categorizing how people have drawn timelines over the course of history to present stories.

I did so upon realizing that most of the relevant visualization research literature has focused on timelines and their uses in data analysis, such as for understanding patterns in electronic health records.

Because of this focus, the expressive range of design choices considered by the research community was far smaller than the range of design choices considered by the design and practice community.

Common Visual Representations of Time

So I just gave you a brief and certainly incomplete primer of different spatial metaphors for time,

and the first dimension in our design space.

Now, before I introduce the second dimension, I want you to consider the following question:

What is a Timeline For?

And that is, what is a timeline for?

Why do we draw these in the first place?

What Happened When?

In what sequence did the events occur?
How long did the events last?
How long between event A and event B?
Did A and B co-occur?
When did A and B occur relative to event C?
Basically, a timeline communicates to the viewer "WHAT HAPPENED WHEN?".

But if we unpack this question, a timeline can answer a number of more detailed questions, like:

In what sequence did events occur? How long were they? Did event A and event B co-occur? And when did they occur relative to some baseline event, say event C?

These questions relate to the second dimension of our design space, and that pertains to time scale.
Visualizing Painters' Lives. © 2013 Accurat.
As an example let's look this set of timelines depicting the lives of notable 20th century painters, by Accurat, Where each lifespan, artistic period, travel, and romantic conquest for each of these painters is positioned according to a common chronological scale.

Here I've redrawn Salvador Dali's biography based on the original Accurat graphic.

You can see his artistic periods like Surrealism and Dada and his travel to places like Paris.

And here is Matisse now shown alongside him. Using a common chronological scale, you notice that were born at different times, and you can see when their respective Cubist and Abstract periods took place and how they might have influenced one another.

When I change the scale to one relative their birth dates, you can now more easily compare the age at which they started their art career, how long they lived; and point out similarities like how they both traveled to Paris in their early 30s,

or I can filter this data and remove the aspect of chronological duration altogether, retaining just a sequential ordering of their artistic periods.

Ultimately, each of these transformations resulted in a different timeline, telling a different story.

Expressive Storytelling with Timelines

Support for chronological or non-chronological narratives,

Choice of representations for time,

Choice of time scales.

Existing tools also tend towards a chronological narrative: either the whole timeline is shown up front, so readers are likely to begin at the start of the timeline, or events are revealed in chronological order.

For some stories, a chronological introduction of events makes sense, while for others it does not, like in my painters example, in which I revealed the career of Matisse later, adding context to the careers of other painters.

To achieve this expressive narrative design, we can make use of animation, highlighting, and annotation to incrementally reveal parts of a narrative and make new comparisons.

Evaluating Data-Driven Stories

Chapter 11: Evaluating Data-Driven Stories & Storytelling Tools.
Amini*, Brehmer* (equal contribution), Bolduan, Elmer, and Wiederkehr.
In Data-Driven Storytelling (CRC Press, 2018)
I've touched upon the evaluation of Timeline Storyteller as an expressive information design tool, but how do we evaluate the quality of the data-driven stories that it creates?

Or how do we evaluate data-driven stories more generally?

A story that makes use of visualization, text, and potentially interaction may incorporate many design choices, in that the author was highly expressive; but was it good?

These questions led to a discussion that culminated in a book chapter that I contributed to in last year's Data-Driven Storytelling book, where my co-authors included a journalist and an information designer.

Evaluating Data-Driven Stories

These questions also served as the underlying basis for the PacificVis Visual Storytelling Contest, which I co-organized in 2017 and 2018.

Both years, I asked a panel of visualization practitioners to judge stories about data submitted by students.

Many of the entries included highly expressive visualization design choices, but essentially they were custom dashboards that told no story, or they expected the viewer to infer the story.

Ultimately, the best entries from these contests reveal that there needs to be a balance between visualization, sequence, narrative, and textual explanation to tell a good story.

Evaluating Data-Driven Storytelling (Tools)

The implication here is that our expressive information design tools should accommodate or at least anticipate the need for each of these elements, and that tools for highly expressive visualization are not enough to tell a good story.

A Crowdsourced Experiment on Mobile Phones
image: flickr/newkemall (cc by)
The answer to how many ranges can be fit into mobile display and to whether you should use a linear or radial layout is of course, "it depends on the data and the task".

We know this because we conducted a crowdsourced experiment in which we asked crowd workers to perform a set of different tasks on their mobile phone.
Our experiment involved both linear and radial layouts, as well as three granularities of time: a week of 7 seven ranges, a month of up to 31 ranges, and a year of 365 ranges.
Data: Temperature (Left) and Sleep (Right) Ranges

Given the prevalence of ranges in weather and sleep tracking applications, we opted to use a year of daily temperature range data, as well as a year of real bedtime and waking time data.

For the temperature data we had observed as well as recorded average temperatures (we show the latter in grey, with the color gradient observed temperature range superimposed over it). However, with sleep data, we only had the observed sleep time ranges.

We recruited 100 participants from Mechanical Turk's U.S. crowd worker population, split evenly into a temperature range group and a sleep range group, though we excluded results from 13 participants, due to non-completion, non-compliance, or for failing to correctly respond to quality control trials distributed throughout the experiment

They had to use a mobile phone running a recent version of iOS or Android.

We calculated ratios in completion time and differences in error rate between the two layouts and between the 3 levels of granularity.

Radial vs. Linear: Task Completion Time Results

People tend to complete comparison tasks in about the same amount of time with either layout, but for tasks that require locating values, people tend to be slower with radial layouts, irrespective of the source of the data.

Radial vs. Linear: Task Accuracy Results

However, radial layouts don't seem to incur accuracy costs, at least with temperature range data, where there tends to be a lot of seasonal variation.

With sleep range data, which exhibits no seasonal variation, people are less accurate when reading values for specific dates or when locating extreme values.

Interpreting Radial vs. Linear Performance Differences

Note: dimensions of an iPhone 6 (w = 375 pt) with a device-pixel ratio of 2x and 12.5% margins
The difficulty in reading range values for indicated dates may be due to the fact that the quantitative domain in a radial layout is compressed to half of the chart area, from the center to the periphery, thus putting it at a disadvantage relative to a linear layout, where the quantitative domain spans the entire height of the chart, so individual marks are twice as tall.

We kept the chart size constant between the two layout conditions, but it would be interesting to repeat our experiment in which mark size is kept constant instead of chart size, meaning that linear range charts would be compressed to half of their current height.

Radial or Linear?

People are, in general, slower with Radial layouts.

Only in some data + task contexts are people less accurate.

(Reading and locating values in the absence of seasonal variation)


People prefer (and are more confident with) Linear layouts
So you might be wondering if our results cast doubt on radial layouts in general, this despite their popularity in practice and design communities.

It's tempting to say this but our results are somewhat more nuanced.

Yes, people tend to be slower with radial layouts, but only in value reading task contexts are they less accurate, while there seems to be no difference in accuracy for comparison tasks.

However, it's also worth remarking that participants universally preferred linear layouts, and they felt more confident using linear layouts relative to radial ones.
Our other primary question in this work is how many ranges can you fit in a mobile display and still retain reasonable task performance, such as when you jump from a week of 7 ranges to a month of 31 ranges to a year of 365.

You might expect that adding more marks in a chart will incur worse performance, but again our findings are more nuanced.

Week vs. Month vs. Year: Task Completion Time Results

As you might expect, people were slower with a month ranges than with a week of ranges,

and in some instances the ratios in completion time from a week to a month was greater than from a month to a year.

Week vs. Month vs. Year: Task Accuracy Time Results

With regards to accuracy, the jump from a week to a month only incurred noticeably worse performance when locating extreme values.

Interestingly, there were cases where people were as accurate or even more accurate with a year of ranges than with a month of ranges, particularly among those looking for extreme temperatures.

Week vs. Month vs. Year: Differences by Group

It is in this respect that we observed different results between the temperature and sleep range groups, where the expected annual trend of temperature ranges appears to make the task easier relative to examining a month of sleep ranges.
Temperature and Sleep Don't Follow Monthly Cycles
One interpretation of our results is that a month may not be an appropriate granularity for either temperature ranges or sleep ranges, since temperatures follow an annual cycle, not a monthly one, and we tend to have a weekly sleep routine across weekdays and weekends, as opposed to a monthly sleep routine.

What's interesting here is that many health and sleep tracking apps have a month or 4-week time window setting.

Selecting an Appropriate Layout and Granularity

Is a cycle meaningful in the context of the data?

Is the task involve locating values? Or comparing them?

Is efficiency the primary consideration?


Locating values (quickly)? → Linear

Comparing values (unconcerned with efficiency)? → Radial or Linear

 video: flickr/50663863@N02 (cc by)
Ultimately, the questions of which layout and which granularity to display are really questions about congruence with the data and task.

You should ask whether a cycle is meaningful in the context of the data, whether the task is about locating values or comparing values, and whether task efficiency is the first priority.

Our experiment was conducted exclusively on mobile phones and we can only speak to that form factor.

Opportunities for Expressive Information Design
A RESEARCH PROGRAM

What I've spoken about today can be situated within a larger effort across the research community to democratize the visualization of data.

Democratizing Data Visualization

This process involves giving more people the ability to visualize their data: to take a process that would have previously required programming expertise, visual design expertise, and / or ample amounts of time, and instead offer an interactive experience that provides both expressivity and design guidance.

The groups of people that I hope to work with here include local and small- to mid-sized newsrooms that don't have dedicated visualization staff, educators preparing lecture content, and curators of cultural institutions and digital collections.

Expressive Information Design and Evolving Technology

I also speculate about the future of human-information interaction in the next decade and what this means for information visualizers as well as consumers, particularly when more of this interaction is occurring in a post-mouse+keyboard world, with mobile and multimodal interaction, and with mixed reality.

As these technologies media evolve, the challenge will be how we can provide discoverable, useful, and enjoyable interactions with this content.

Expressive Information Design and
The Attention Economy

I'll close with outlining some of the broader opportunities for the future of data visualization and expressive information design that stem from current challenges in our society.

How do we ensure that the information presented these stories is memorable? Or personally relevant?

We need to collect both good and bad examples, distill design patterns, and comparatively test them, both in the lab and in the wild.

Expressive Information Design to Boost Graphicacy

A second opportunity pertains to the combination of data literacy and visual literacy, or what journalist and author Alberto Cairo refers to as "graphicacy".

Here there are opportunities to innovate in annotation, the pairing of data-bound visual elements with an annotation layer, with adjacent textual explanations, particularly in the context of uncertainty in the data, or with complementary visual forms that the viewer can use as scaffolding when learning new ones.

Expressive Information Design for Decision Support

The last opportunity is about providing contextually-appropriate information to support personal decisions with respect to health, finance, civic engagement, and resource conservation.

How should this vital information be revealed in the moment, or on a mobile or wearable device, and can we understand the long-term effects of using such information aids?
